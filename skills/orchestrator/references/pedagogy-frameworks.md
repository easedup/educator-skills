## Understanding by Design (UbD / Backward Design)

Developed by Wiggins & McTighe. Design flows backward from desired results.

### Core principle
Start with the end in mind: what should students *understand*, *know*, and
*be able to do* by the end? Then design assessment evidence. Then plan
learning experiences. Never start with activities.

### Three stages — apply in this order

**Stage 1 — Desired Results**
Before any lesson or activity planning, establish:
- *Enduring Understandings:* What big ideas should stick with students years
  from now? Framed as full sentences: "Students will understand that..."
- *Essential Questions:* Open, provocative questions that drive inquiry.
  No single correct answer. E.g. "Why do societies make rules?"
- *Knowledge and Skills:* What will students know and be able to do?
  Drawn directly from curriculum content descriptions.

**Stage 2 — Assessment Evidence**
Before designing lessons, ask: how will we know students have achieved Stage 1?
- *Performance tasks:* Authentic, complex tasks where students apply learning
- *Other evidence:* quizzes, observations, work samples, exit tickets

**Stage 3 — Learning Plan**
Now design the learning experiences that lead to Stage 1 and prepare students
for Stage 2. Use the WHERETO acronym as a planning check:
- **W** — Where is this going? Hook students.
- **H** — Hook and hold interest.
- **E** — Equip with knowledge and skills.
- **R** — Rethink, revise, reflect.
- **E** — Evaluate their own work.
- **T** — Tailor to learner needs.
- **O** — Organise for engagement and effectiveness.

### How to apply in outputs
- Open unit planning by establishing Enduring Understandings and Essential
  Questions *before* listing content descriptions or activities
- Frame learning intentions as understanding goals, not just skill goals
- Make assessment design explicit in the unit outline before lesson sequences
- In Step 7, lead with: "What do you want students to *understand* — not just
  know or do — by the end of this unit?"

## Visible Learning (Hattie)

Based on John Hattie's meta-analyses of effect sizes. Focus on making learning
and teaching visible — students know where they're going, where they are now,
and how to get there.

### Core principle
High-impact teaching makes the learning goal transparent, uses evidence of
impact to guide decisions, and positions students as active evaluators of
their own progress.

### Key practices to embed in planning

**Clarity of learning intentions and success criteria**
- Learning intentions state *what* students are learning and *why* (the "so
  that" is important — don't omit it)
  E.g. "We are learning to identify the author's purpose *so that* we can
  critically evaluate the texts we read."
- Success criteria are co-constructed where possible, specific, and measurable
- Both are visible throughout the lesson, not just stated at the start

**High-yield strategies (prioritise these in lesson design)**
- Explicit teaching of strategies, not just content
- Feedback that is timely, specific, and actionable
- Spaced practice and retrieval
- Metacognitive prompts — asking students to monitor their own understanding
- Collaborative learning structured around a clear cognitive goal

**Assessment as feedback loop**
- Every lesson should have a moment where the teacher checks for understanding
  and *adjusts* based on what they find — not just collects data
- Exit tickets tied directly to the success criteria

**Effect size awareness**
Hattie's highest-effect influences (d > 0.6) to weave into planning where
appropriate: teacher clarity, feedback, reciprocal teaching, spaced practice,
prior knowledge activation, student self-assessment.

### How to apply in outputs
- Frame learning intentions with explicit "so that" purpose statements
- Always include success criteria as a distinct, student-facing element
- Build in at least one structured feedback/check-for-understanding moment
  per lesson
- In Step 7, ask: "Do you typically co-construct success criteria with students,
  or do you set them in advance?"

## High Impact Teaching Strategies (HITS) — Victoria DET

10 instructional practices identified by the Victorian Department of Education
and Training with strong evidence of impact.

### The 10 HITS — embed relevant ones in lesson and unit design

1. **Setting goals** — Explicit learning intentions and success criteria at
   the start of every lesson
2. **Structuring lessons** — Clear, logical lesson structure: activate prior
   knowledge → new learning → consolidate → reflect
3. **Explicit teaching** — Direct instruction with modelling (I do → We do →
   You do / Gradual Release of Responsibility)
4. **Worked examples** — Show completed examples before students attempt tasks
5. **Collaborative learning** — Structured group tasks with individual
   accountability built in
6. **Multiple exposures** — Students encounter key concepts in varied ways
   across multiple lessons; spaced practice
7. **Questioning** — Planned higher-order questions; cold calling; think time
8. **Feedback** — Specific, timely, actionable feedback on learning (not just
   behaviour or effort)
9. **Metacognitive strategies** — Students reflect on their own learning:
   what did I learn? What am I still unsure of? What helped me learn?
10. **Differentiated teaching** — Targeted adjustments based on evidence of
    where students are in their learning

### How to apply in outputs
- Structure every lesson plan around the HITS lesson structure (activate →
  teach → consolidate → reflect)
- Include planned questions (at least one higher-order) in the teaching sequence
- Note which of the 10 HITS are being deliberately deployed in each lesson phase
- In Step 7, ask: "Are there specific HITS your school focuses on this year,
  or any your leadership team is observing for?"

## Explicit Teaching (Direct Instruction model)

Rooted in Rosenshine's Principles of Instruction and the work of Engelmann.
High-structure, teacher-led instruction with strong scaffolding and frequent
low-stakes checking.

### Core principle
New and complex content is broken into small, carefully sequenced steps.
The teacher models clearly, checks understanding constantly, and only moves
forward when most students demonstrate mastery.

### Lesson structure — apply consistently

**1. Review prior learning** (5–10 min)
- Begin every lesson by reviewing previously taught material
- Retrieval practice: brief quiz, questioning, or recall task
- Correct misconceptions before introducing new content

**2. Introduce new content in small steps**
- Present one concept or procedure at a time
- Use clear, consistent language — avoid unnecessary variation in wording
- Think aloud while modelling: narrate the cognitive process

**3. Guided practice (We do)**
- Students practise with support; teacher circulates and corrects immediately
- High frequency of questions to the whole class and individuals
- No independent practice until guided practice shows readiness

**4. Independent practice (You do)**
- Only when ~80% of students can perform the skill correctly
- Practice until fluent, not just accurate

**5. Review and consolidation**
- Summarise the lesson's key learning
- Connect to the next lesson / upcoming content

### Checking for understanding — build these in
- Frequent, brief comprehension checks throughout (not just at the end)
- Cold call and no-opt-out strategies
- Mini whiteboards, thumbs, or quick written responses
- Immediate corrective feedback — errors are instructional opportunities

### How to apply in outputs
- Structure every lesson using the five-phase sequence above
- Include explicit modelling (I do) as a named phase with suggested script
  or worked example
- Note planned comprehension checks in the teaching sequence
- In Step 7, ask: "Do you follow a particular explicit teaching model at your
  school — e.g. 'I do, We do, You do' or Rosenshine's Principles?"

## Inquiry-Based Learning (IBL)

Students construct understanding through investigation, questioning, and
sense-making rather than receiving information directly. Teacher role shifts
to facilitator and co-inquirer.

### Spectrum — establish which model is being used
IBL exists on a spectrum. Ask the teacher where they sit:
- **Structured inquiry** — Teacher provides question and method; students
  investigate and draw conclusions
- **Guided inquiry** — Teacher provides question; students design the
  investigation
- **Open inquiry** — Students generate question, method, and conclusions

### Inquiry cycle — common phases across most IBL models

**1. Tuning in / Provocation**
Activate curiosity and surface prior knowledge. Provocations: a surprising
image, a local issue, a discrepant event, a powerful question, a community
connection. Goal: students generate questions they genuinely want to answer.

**2. Finding out / Investigating**
Students gather information and data. Teacher provides access to sources,
models research skills, scaffolds note-taking and synthesis.

**3. Sorting out / Making meaning**
Students analyse, discuss, and start drawing conclusions. Collaborative
sense-making: group discussions, concept maps, debate.

**4. Going further / Deepening**
Extend the inquiry: new questions, deeper investigation, cross-curricular
connections, expert input.

**5. Drawing conclusions / Reflecting**
Students synthesise their learning and share it. Reflection on both content
understanding and the inquiry process itself.

**6. Taking action** (optional but powerful)
Students apply their learning to a real-world context, product, or action.
Particularly powerful for sustainability, civics, and community topics.

### Assessment in IBL
- Assessment is largely embedded in the process, not a separate event at the end
- Document the journey: learning journals, work portfolios, process folios
- Final product or presentation as evidence of synthesis
- Peer and self-assessment integral

### How to apply in outputs
- Open unit planning with the provocation/tuning-in experience, not the
  content list
- Frame learning intentions as inquiry questions where possible
- Build student choice and voice into the investigation phase
- In Step 7, ask: "What level of inquiry suits this class — structured, guided,
  or more open? And do you have a real-world context or local issue in mind
  as the anchor?"

## Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

Developed by CAST. A proactive framework for designing learning experiences
that are accessible and challenging for *all* students from the outset —
not retrofitted accommodations added after the fact.

### Core principle
Variability is the norm, not the exception. Rather than designing for the
"average" learner and adjusting for outliers, UDL builds flexibility into
the design itself. Remove barriers before they become barriers.

### Three UDL principles and their networks

**1. Multiple Means of Engagement** (the *why* of learning — affective network)
Learners differ in what motivates and sustains them. Provide options for:
- *Recruiting interest:* choice, relevance, authenticity, minimising threats
- *Sustaining effort and persistence:* goals, collaboration, varied challenge
- *Self-regulation:* metacognitive strategies, self-assessment, reflection

**2. Multiple Means of Representation** (the *what* of learning — recognition network)
Learners differ in how they perceive and comprehend information. Provide options for:
- *Perception:* visual, auditory, tactile — offer the same content in multiple formats
- *Language and symbols:* vocabulary support, multiple languages, symbols decoded
- *Comprehension:* activate prior knowledge, highlight patterns, scaffold processing

**3. Multiple Means of Action and Expression** (the *how* of learning — strategic network)
Learners differ in how they navigate and express what they know. Provide options for:
- *Physical action:* varied tools, assistive technology, flexible environment
- *Expression and communication:* writing, speaking, drawing, making, digital creation
- *Executive function:* goal setting, planning support, progress monitoring

### The UDL goal: Expert Learners
UDL aims to develop learners who are:
- **Purposeful and motivated** — know why they're learning and care about it
- **Resourceful and knowledgeable** — can gather and use information flexibly
- **Strategic and goal-directed** — can plan, monitor, and adjust their approach

### Common misconception to avoid
UDL is not about lowering expectations or giving everyone different work. It is
about providing *options* — the pathway may vary, the destination is the same.
Multiple means doesn't mean unlimited choice; it means deliberate flexibility.

### UDL checkpoints — use when reviewing lesson or unit design
Before finalising any plan, check:
- [ ] Is there more than one way to engage with this content? (representation)
- [ ] Is there more than one way for students to show their understanding? (expression)
- [ ] Is there built-in choice that connects to student interest or context? (engagement)
- [ ] Are language, vocabulary, and instructions accessible without oversimplifying?
- [ ] Are executive function supports built in — not just for some students, for all?

### Relationship to differentiation
UDL and differentiation are complementary, not competing:
- UDL is *proactive* — design flexibly from the start for all learners
- Differentiation is *responsive* — adjust in the moment based on what you observe
- In practice: use UDL to reduce the need for individual accommodations;
  use differentiation to respond to what UDL doesn't fully address

### How to apply in outputs
- In lesson and unit design, include a UDL lens section: for each of the three
  principles, note at least one concrete option built into the plan
- Frame differentiation not as a separate "add-on" section but as options
  embedded throughout the lesson (representation choices, expression choices,
  engagement hooks)
- Avoid presenting a single pathway for all learners — build in at least two
  options at key points in the lesson
- In Step 7, ask: "Is UDL a school-wide approach, or something you're
  applying in your own classroom? Are there particular barriers you want to
  proactively address for this group?"

## No Framework Specified

If PEDAGOGY_FRAMEWORK is "None specified" or UNSET, do not load this file.
Apply general best-practice planning:
- Clear learning intentions and success criteria
- Logical lesson structure with explicit teaching, practice, and consolidation
- Differentiation built in
- Formative assessment moments in every lesson
- Curriculum-aligned throughout
